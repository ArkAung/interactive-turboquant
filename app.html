A first-principles walk-through

Compressing AI vectors to 3 bits per number
without losing accuracy.

Modern language models store enormous tables of high-dimensional vectors — KV caches, embeddings, attention keys. TurboQuant is a surprisingly elegant recipe that compresses each coordinate of these vectors to 2–4 bits, with provably near-optimal distortion, zero memory overhead for scale factors, and no training or calibration. This page walks through why it works, starting from the bottom.

TurboQuant: Online VQ with Near-optimal Distortion

The single idea you will take away: in high dimensions, a random rotation turns every vector into a "nice" one — so you can pre-compute one universal codebook and use it forever. Everything else is bookkeeping.

§0.3 · Mean Squared Error
Why we square the mistake.
“Error” is how far off you are. If we just averaged the sign, positive and negative errors would cancel. Squaring makes every miss positive and punishes big misses quadratically — so the best guess becomes the average. Move the guess; watch the MSE.

§0.4 · Unbiased vs Biased Estimator
Noisy is fine. Systematically wrong is not.
An estimator is a recipe that produces a guess $\hat\theta$ for a truth $\theta$. It is unbiased if the average of the guesses equals $\theta$: $\mathbb{E}[\hat\theta]=\theta$. Shots can scatter wildly and still be unbiased — as long as the center lands on the bullseye. Click fire 50 shots.

A rigid spin — preserves lengths and angles.

§0.6 · Where bell-curves come from (CLT)
Add up many small randoms → Gaussian. Always.
The Central Limit Theorem: sum many independent random numbers and their distribution always converges to a bell curve. It doesn't matter if each piece is a coin flip, a uniform roll, or anything else. This is why a rotated coordinate behaves like a Gaussian.

§0.8 · Quantization, in one dimension
Snap every number to the nearest of $2^b$ levels.
This is what “$b$ bits per number” means. With $b=2$ you get 4 levels, $b=3$ gives 8. The gap between levels is your worst-case error. Doubling bits quarters the gap — the source of the magic “$4^{-b}$” you will see later.

§1 · Setup

What is vector quantization, really?

You have a vector $\mathbf{x}\in\mathbb{R}^d$ — say $d{=}1536$ floats from an OpenAI embedding. You want to store it using just $b$ bits per coordinate (total $b\cdot d$ bits), then later recover an approximation $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}$ that is close to $\mathbf{x}$. "Close" is measured by

MSE distortion   $D_{\text{mse}} = \mathbb{E}\big[\,\|\mathbf{x} - \tilde{\mathbf{x}}\|_2^2\,\big]$    or    inner-product error   $D_{\text{prod}} = \mathbb{E}\big[\,|\langle\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}\rangle - \langle\mathbf{y},\tilde{\mathbf{x}}\rangle|^2\,\big]$

The second one matters because attention scores and nearest-neighbor queries are all inner products. We would like the estimator to be unbiased: $\mathbb{E}[\langle\mathbf{y},\tilde{\mathbf{x}}\rangle] = \langle\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}\rangle$.

■ DECODER · KEY WORDSplain English

MSE distortion: averaged squared miss between the true vector and its reconstruction — primer §0.3.

Inner product $\langle y, x\rangle$: how much the two vectors point the same way — primer §0.2. This is the thing attention actually computes.

Estimator: a rule (here: quantize, then decode) that spits out an approximation $\hat s$ of some true number $s$.

Unbiased estimator: across many queries, the average of $\hat s$ equals $s$. The individual shots can be noisy; the cluster center lands on truth. Primer §0.4 is this idea, with a bullseye.

§2 · Worst-case vectors hurt

The adversarial coordinate, and why production systems pay a tax

A real embedding rarely looks perfectly flat. Trained models famously produce outlier channels — a handful of coordinates whose magnitudes dwarf the rest. Any fixed $[-L, L]$ grid either clips the outliers or wastes resolution on the bulk. So production quantizers — GPTQ, AWQ, KIVI, KVQuant — do the natural thing: compute $(\min, \max)$ (or zero-point & scale) for every small block and store those in full precision as side information.

The catch. Storing a float16 scale + zero-point for every 16–64-element block adds 1–2 extra bits per quantized number. At a 3-bit budget that is a 33–66% overhead. You are compressing, but dragging a pile of bookkeeping along with it. TurboQuant's explicit goal is to eliminate this overhead while still handling worst-case vectors.

The data-oblivious requirement makes it even harder: we want a procedure that works for any vector without first looking at a training set. No fine-tuning. No dataset-specific calibration. Just a fixed recipe. How can a fixed recipe possibly do well against a spike vector?

§3 · The core trick

Multiply by a random rotation. Watch the spike dissolve.

Pick a random orthogonal matrix $\boldsymbol{\Pi}\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$ — concretely, generate a Gaussian matrix, then run QR decomposition to orthonormalize it. Now replace $\mathbf{x}$ with $\mathbf{y} = \boldsymbol{\Pi}\mathbf{x}$. Because $\boldsymbol{\Pi}$ is a rotation it preserves every geometric property we care about:

$\|\boldsymbol{\Pi}\mathbf{x}\|_2 = \|\mathbf{x}\|_2$  ·  $\langle \boldsymbol{\Pi}\mathbf{x},\,\boldsymbol{\Pi}\mathbf{y}\rangle = \langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle$  ·  $\boldsymbol{\Pi}^{\!\top}\boldsymbol{\Pi} = \mathbf{I}$

So rotating, quantizing, then rotating back is lossless at the rotation step. The quantization error we pay is only on the rotated vector's coordinates. And that is exactly where rotation buys us something.

The "aha" in one sentence. A rotation does not create or destroy information — it redistributes it. An adversarial vector with all its mass in one coordinate is, after rotation, a typical vector with mass spread evenly. The quantizer only sees the typical vector.
§4 · Why the rotation works

Coordinates of random unit vectors are nearly Gaussian.

Here is the gorgeous fact that makes the whole paper tick. Sample a point uniformly from the sphere $\mathbb{S}^{d-1}$. What is the distribution of a single coordinate $x_j$? Lemma 1 of the paper gives the exact density:

$f_X(x) \;=\; \dfrac{\Gamma(d/2)}{\sqrt{\pi}\,\Gamma((d-1)/2)}\,(1-x^2)^{(d-3)/2}\,,\quad x\in[-1,1]$
— a scaled/shifted Beta distribution.

As $d$ grows this distribution looks more and more like a narrow bell curve. In fact by the central limit theorem (and a slightly subtle argument about independence on the sphere) it converges to $\mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\,\tfrac{1}{d}\right)$. Rotating any fixed $\mathbf{x}$ with a random $\boldsymbol{\Pi}$ is equivalent to sampling a random point on a sphere of the same radius — so $(\boldsymbol{\Pi}\mathbf{x})_j$ inherits this near-Gaussian distribution, no matter what $\mathbf{x}$ was.

Consequence for quantization. Every coordinate of the rotated vector has the same, known distribution. We can solve the scalar quantization problem for $\mathcal{N}(0, 1/d)$ once, store the optimal codebook, and reuse it for every coordinate of every vector forever. No per-block scale factors. Zero overhead.
§5 · The universal codebook

Lloyd–Max: the optimal partition of a known distribution.

Problem: given a PDF $f_X$, partition $\mathbb{R}$ into $2^b$ cells with centroids $c_1 \le \dots \le c_{2^b}$ to minimize expected squared error $\int (x - c_{i(x)})^2 f_X(x)\,dx$. The solution (Lloyd 1982, Max 1960) alternates two steps:

  • Assignment: each centroid owns the Voronoi cell around it — boundaries are midpoints between adjacent centroids.
  • Update: each centroid moves to the conditional mean of its cell: $c_k \leftarrow \mathbb{E}[X \mid X \in \text{cell}_k]$.

Repeat until stable. Because we know $f_X$ analytically (the Beta density above), we compute these once, for $b=1,2,3,4,5,6$, and bake them into a tiny lookup table.

§7 · A subtle problem

MSE-optimal quantizers are biased for inner products.

■ RECALL · §0.4Bullseye test

“Unbiased” is the bullseye-scatter picture from the primer: shots may wander, but their average lands on the truth. “Biased” means the cluster center is parked somewhere else — no amount of averaging saves you. Everything in this section is about: our attention scores are a cluster that is systematically parked too low.

Attention does not want $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}$; it wants $\langle \mathbf{q}, \mathbf{k}\rangle$. And it turns out that an MSE-optimal quantizer, while minimizing pointwise squared error, systematically underestimates inner products. The reason is that rounding toward a centroid biases the vector length — the projection onto any query direction shrinks by a predictable factor.

Where does $2/\pi$ come from?  For a standard Gaussian $g$, $\mathbb{E}[|g|]=\sqrt{2/\pi}$ — the “half-normal” mean. The 1-bit MSE codebook rounds each rotated coordinate to $\pm\sqrt{2/\pi}/\sqrt d$; when you dot-product that reconstruction back against $\mathbf{y}$, you pick up another $\sqrt{2/\pi}$ factor in expectation. Multiply: $2/\pi \approx 0.637$. That is the entire bias, from first principles.

The easiest case to see: $b{=}1$. The optimal MSE codebook is $\{-\sqrt{2/\pi}/\sqrt{d},\,+\sqrt{2/\pi}/\sqrt{d}\}$, so $Q(\mathbf{x}) = \sqrt{2/(\pi d)}\cdot \operatorname{sign}(\boldsymbol{\Pi}\mathbf{x})$. A calculation gives

$\mathbb{E}\big[\langle\mathbf{y},\tilde{\mathbf{x}}\rangle\big] \;=\; \dfrac{2}{\pi}\cdot\langle\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}\rangle \;\ne\; \langle\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}\rangle.$

That $2/\pi \approx 0.637$ is a multiplicative bias. It shrinks as $b$ grows, but it never goes to zero cleanly — and for low-bit, high-dimensional settings (exactly where you want to be) the bias is painfully real.

§8 · The un-biaser

QJL — squeezing unbiased inner products out of 1 bit per coordinate.

■ INTUITIONUn-bias by construction

Step back: the bias we just measured is a known number, $\sqrt{2/\pi}$ per application of sign-then-dot. If it is known, we can cancel it. That is exactly what QJL does — it multiplies by $\sqrt{\pi/2}/d$ on the decode side so the shrinkage cancels exactly. The quantization stays lossy (it is still 1 bit!), but the average estimate lands on the bullseye.

This is the primer's “noisy but unbiased” picture literally playing out: each individual estimate can be far off, but 3000 of them average to the right answer.

The Quantized Johnson-Lindenstrauss transform is the second ingredient. Let $\mathbf{S}\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}$ have i.i.d. $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ entries (a fresh random Gaussian matrix). Define the 1-bit encoding and its decoder

$Q_{\text{jl}}(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \operatorname{sign}(\mathbf{S}\mathbf{x}) \in\{-1,+1\}^d,\quad Q_{\text{jl}}^{-1}(\mathbf{z}) \;=\; \dfrac{\sqrt{\pi/2}}{d}\cdot\mathbf{S}^{\!\top}\mathbf{z}.$

One bit per coordinate. No scales, no side info — same as before. But this one is unbiased for inner products, exactly:

$\mathbb{E}\big[\langle \mathbf{y}, Q_{\text{jl}}^{-1}(Q_{\text{jl}}(\mathbf{x}))\rangle\big] \;=\; \langle\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}\rangle.$

The magic that makes it work: for a standard Gaussian $g$, $\mathbb{E}[|g|]=\sqrt{2/\pi}$. The $\sqrt{\pi/2}$ pre-factor cancels that shrinkage exactly, leaving a clean, unbiased estimator whose variance is $\le \tfrac{\pi}{2d}\|\mathbf{y}\|^2$ (Lemma 4 in the paper).

Putting it all together: TurboQuant-prod

For a $b$-bit inner-product quantizer:

  1. Rotate $\mathbf{x}\to \boldsymbol{\Pi}\mathbf{x}$ as before.
  2. Apply $b{-}1$-bit MSE-optimal quantization. Call the result $\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{\text{mse}}$.
  3. Compute the residual $\mathbf{r} = \boldsymbol{\Pi}\mathbf{x} - \tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{\text{mse}}$ and quantize it with a single bit of QJL: store $\operatorname{sign}(\mathbf{S}\mathbf{r})$ and the residual norm $\|\mathbf{r}\|$.
  4. Decode: $\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = \boldsymbol{\Pi}^{\!\top}\big(\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{\text{mse}} + \|\mathbf{r}\|\cdot \tfrac{\sqrt{\pi/2}}{d}\mathbf{S}^{\!\top}\operatorname{sign}(\mathbf{S}\mathbf{r})\big)$.

The first stage nails the magnitude; the 1 bit of QJL on the leftover residual provides the un-biasing. Total cost: still $b$ bits per coordinate. Inner products are exactly unbiased, and the variance is provably small (Theorem 2).

§9 · Hitting Shannon's floor

How close is TurboQuant to the theoretical best?

Using Shannon's lossy source-coding theorem (via Yao's minimax principle) the paper proves that no quantizer — randomized, adaptive, data-dependent, anything — can do better than $D_{\text{mse}} \ge 4^{-b}$ for worst-case inputs on the unit sphere. TurboQuant's upper bound is $\tfrac{\sqrt{3}\pi}{2}\cdot 4^{-b}$, which is within a factor of ≈ 2.7 of this information-theoretic floor — and the factor shrinks below 1.5 at $b{=}1$.

The exponential improvement over older methods

Prior data-oblivious quantizers like uniform rounding or scalar sketches give error that decays polynomially in the bit budget (e.g. $\mathcal{O}(1/b)$). TurboQuant's $4^{-b}$ is exponentially better in bit-width, which is what lets KV caches go from 16-bit → 3-bit with zero downstream quality loss.

Zoomed-out summary — all three papers together

  1. QJL (2024). Take the sign of a random Gaussian projection. One bit per coordinate, unbiased inner product, zero overhead. The un-biaser primitive.
  2. PolarQuant (Feb 2025). After random pre-conditioning, convert to polar coordinates. Radii are concentrated; angles are concentrated around $\pi/4$. Quantize those well-concentrated distributions independently. An alternative geometry that also avoids side-info.
  3. TurboQuant (Apr 2025). Unifies and strengthens the above: pre-rotation + Beta coordinates + optimal Lloyd-Max codebook (for MSE) + QJL on residual (for unbiasedness). Provably near-Shannon, fully GPU-parallel, online.
The whole trick, in one line. Random rotations make every vector "look Gaussian" in its coordinates — so one fixed, optimal codebook works for all of them. That collapses the vector-quantization problem into the best-studied scalar one.
